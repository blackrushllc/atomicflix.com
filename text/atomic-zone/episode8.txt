Our story begins at the bedside of a dying man.

He is impossibly old. Far older than any man has a right to be. His hair is white. His skin is thin with age. His voice is weak, but his eyes still carry the strange authority of a man who once shaped the destiny of the world.

Around him stands a family that should not exist.

Great-great-grandchildren in elegant retro-futurist clothing. Doctors with polished instruments and solemn faces. A room full of curved glass, shining metal, and strange art-deco machines. Beyond the windows lies the year 2037, not as we know it, but as people long ago imagined it would be. A radiant world of silver towers, sky lanes full of aircraft, gleaming monorails, and a civilization built not on war and ruin, but on beauty, invention, and peace.

The mysterious dying old man looks at them with the sorrow of a man who has already lost everything.

Then he begins to speak.

This is not the real world, he tells them.

It is only the world he made.

Long ago, he says, he came here from another 2037. Not this shining, dreamlike future, but a darker one. A harsher one. A world of wars, dictators, revolutions, camps, bombers, mushroom clouds, and fear. A century stained by blood and terror. A world that climbed into the stars only after dragging half of hell along with it.

No one in the room understands him.

No one believes him.

But the mysterious dying old man continues.

He says that in that other 2037, he had once been a young test pilot. A brave and handsome man chosen to risk himself in dangerous experiments. He had been sent to France to test a time machine.

The rules of the machine were simple.

He could travel into the past and remain there only so long as he kept a special watch strapped to his wrist.

If he pressed the return button on the watch, he would be pulled back to the present.

If the watch were broken, removed, or lost, he would be pulled back automatically.

And if he died, the same thing would happen.

Everything he had changed in the past would reverse itself, all the way back to the moment he arrived.

Time would unwind like a reel of film.

The past would heal over.

The present would be restored.

He was never meant to stay.

He was only meant to observe.

And then the old man closes his eyes, and memory carries us backward.

We see the young test pilot appear in Normandy, France, in the spring of 1912.

The air is soft. The grass is green. Horse carts roll along the roads. Men wear dark coats. Women wear hats. The old world still believes itself safe.

And there, almost at once, he sees her.

A beautiful young woman. Intelligent. Elegant. Striking. The kind of woman who seems at once unfamiliar and inevitable, as if all of history had been holding its breath for her entrance.

He speaks to her.

He walks with her.

He laughs with her.

And almost immediately, something impossible happens.

He falls in love.

Not ordinary love. Not passing fascination. Not curiosity.

But the sudden, irrational, all-consuming kind of love that ignores time, reason, duty, and consequence.

For the first time since entering the machine, the young test pilot forgets his mission.

Then he learns the truth.

The beautiful young woman is about to sail for America.

Aboard the Titanic.

And the young test pilot knows exactly what that means.

He knows the iceberg.

He knows the cold Atlantic.

He knows the screams in the dark.

And because love has become stronger than duty, he boards the ship.

What follows is the turning of the world.

Aboard the Titanic, among chandeliers and polished brass and the lazy confidence of doomed luxury, the young test pilot changes history. By warning the right people, by acting at the right moment, by using knowledge no man in 1912 should possess, he prevents the sinking.

The Titanic lives.

Thousands survive.

And from that one impossible act, the century breaks in a different direction.

The young test pilot stays in the past.

He marries the beautiful young woman.

He builds a life with her.

And with the knowledge he carried backward from a terrible future, he begins to transform the world.

He gives humanity inventions early.

He speeds the growth of aviation.

He advances science by decades.

He prevents wars before they begin.

He changes alliances before they can harden into hatred.

He turns mankind away from the century of blood that was waiting for it.

And in its place, he helps create a civilization of wonder.

No world wars.

No communist empire.

No century of ashes.

Instead, the Earth becomes a shining retro-futurist dream. Airships drift above cities. Silver aircraft cross oceans in hours. Rocket towers rise into the sky. Robots serve in polished homes. Undersea colonies thrive. Science grows bright and elegant. The world becomes the kind of future pulp magazines once promised to children with wide eyes and hopeful hearts.

And in time, the young test pilot becomes one of the greatest men in history.

A statesman.

A visionary.

A leader of the world.

The mysterious dying old man.

And he did it all for love.

Not for power.

Not for conquest.

Not even for utopia itself.

But because one beautiful young woman in Normandy smiled at him, and he could not bear to let her die aboard a ship history had already condemned.

Now we return to the deathbed.

The mysterious dying old man is fading.

His family leans close.

The doctors exchange worried glances.

The old man tries to explain what none of them can understand.

When he dies, this entire world will vanish.

The towers, the monorails, the rockets, the peace, the family standing beside him, the generations that were born because he stayed, the whole shining century he made — all of it depends on the watch still fastened to his wrist.

He begs the doctors to save him.

Keep him alive.

Do not let him die.

Do not remove the watch.

Perhaps there is some way to preserve his body. Some way to save the hand. Some way to anchor the world a little longer.

But to everyone in the room, it is only the wild talk of a dying old man.

No one understands that he is not begging for his life.

He is begging for theirs.

And so his descendants gather near him in the gentle light of a future city that should never have existed.

They comfort him.

They tell him to rest.

They promise that everything will be all right.

And then he dies.

What follows is not death.

It is erasure.

In a matter of seconds, time tears backward.

The towers unbuild themselves.

The monorails vanish.

The airships disappear.

The rockets descend.

The cities unwind.

The inventions are unborn.

The marriages are undone.

The children are unmade.

The generations vanish.

The whole bright false century collapses in reverse, all the way back through the years, through the saved Titanic, through Normandy, through 1912 itself, until the young test pilot is ripped out of the past and hurled forward into the instant he left.

The laboratory returns.

The real 2037 returns.

The harsher one.

The ordinary one.

The young test pilot stumbles out of the machine, not old now, but young again, strong again, and shattered beyond words.

To the scientists in the room, he has only been gone for a second.

The lab assistant runs to him and asks what happened.

But the young test pilot cannot answer.

He collapses into a chair, covers his face with his hands, and says only the same words again and again.

It’s all gone.

All gone.

All gone.

Then, through his grief, he feels someone helping him.

A hand on his shoulder.

A gentle voice.

He looks up.

And there she is.

The beautiful young woman from Normandy.

The woman from the Titanic.

The woman he loved, married, saved, and spent a lifetime beside.

Only here she is young.

Bright-eyed.

Alive.

Dressed not in the clothes of 1912, but in a sleek time-travel jumpsuit.

She has not gone anywhere yet.

She is in the laboratory because she was meant to be the next test subject. Perhaps, to visit a majestic ship lost to history.

She looks at him with concern, and perhaps with the faintest spark of something else.

Then she leans toward the lab assistant and whispers softly:

Is this the other test pilot you hired?

He’s cute.

I hope he’s okay.

And there, at the end of all ambition, all machinery, all attempts to master time itself, lies the final little joke.

Man was never meant to see the future.

Perhaps that is not our destiny.

Perhaps that is not why we were made.

The future must remain hidden from us, not because it lacks meaning, but because meaning requires uncertainty. We are not built to live by spoilers. We are not meant to know every consequence before it arrives. We must walk forward blind, because hope itself only works in darkness.

But this is the Atomic Zone.

And though the shining world he built is gone...

though the century he tried to escape has returned...

though the life he made for love has been erased like chalk from a board...

love, being older than time and cleverer than machinery, still finds a way.
