# **The Atomic Zone — Episode 3: “The Proxy”**

In this episode of *The Atomic Zone*, greed wears a silk tie, corruption lives in a penthouse office, and death arrives with hydraulic footsteps.

The entire story unfolds inside the lavish executive office suite of Gamora Pharmaceuticals, a palace of polished marble, dark mahogany, brass fixtures, smoked glass, and expensive bad taste. It is the kind of 1980s corporate environment where everything is oversized, theatrical, and faintly sinister — a giant desk like a throne, a long boardroom table fit for conspiracies, a secretary’s station tucked near the entrance, an elevator on one side, a sweeping staircase on the other, and mounted prominently on the wall, a hulking television monitor everyone in this world still calls the Televisor.

Here we meet Nedry, a young corporate prince with the slick vanity and dead eyes of an *American Psycho* type executive, dressed in a perfect pinstripe suit and wearing the permanent expression of a man who mistakes cruelty for strength. At his side is his sister Bella, equally young, equally vicious, looking like an 80s supermodel dressed for a hostile takeover — all sharp cheekbones, bright lipstick, sculpted hair, shoulder pads, and ice in her voice. Together they are the heirs apparent to Gamora Pharmaceuticals, a company so openly corrupt that its private slogans might as well be “Bribery is cheaper than research,” and “Senators are cheaper than scientists.”

Lurking near them is Red, Nedry’s hired muscle, a sadistic street predator wrapped in the expensive colors of corporate security, with the cold grin of a man who enjoys violence almost as much as he enjoys being paid for it. At the secretary’s desk sits Nadine, a timid twenty-year-old office girl with big glasses, poofy hair pinned up too tight, and the frightened, awkward posture of the “ugly duckling” from every 80s movie who would become beautiful if only the world were kinder. She is quiet, pure-hearted, and perpetually terrified of the monsters in suits around her. Nearby, drifting in and out on simple errands, is Junior from the mail room, an autistic young man who seems oddly immune to the social poison of Gamora. He does not understand the hierarchy, the fear, or the malice. He is simply present.

And above them all, though never physically in the room, is the Old Man — the monstrous CEO of Gamora, Nedry and Bella’s grandfather, a ruthless tyrant so foul-tempered and theatrical that every person in his orbit has their own private threat phrase from him. To Nedry, it is, “I’ll rip your guts out!” To Bella, “I’ll tear your head off!” To timid Nadine, “I’m gonna paddle your butt!” These threats are absurd, repetitive, and probably empty, but they have become part of the man’s legend. He rules by intimidation, insult, and fear.

Or at least he did.

Because as the story begins, Nedry’s office is in chaos. The Old Man has suffered a massive stroke and now lies half-comatose in a private medical facility deep in the basement of the building, tended by his personal scientist, Stebner, and surrounded by loyal staff and security. Through sharp, vicious conversation, we learn the truth: this was no natural event. Nedry and Bella, desperate to inherit the company, had poisoned the Old Man, hoping to finish him off and step neatly into power. But the old brute refused to die. As Bella coolly observes, he is the kind of man who would stay alive out of sheer meanness, just to keep tormenting people a little longer.

Nedry is frantic, certain their plan has failed. But Bella, cold and strategic, thinks the old man’s paralysis may be even better than death. Surely a man trapped in a bed cannot run a corporation. Surely now he will be forced to resign.

But then the attorneys and senior executives begin to arrive.

The board assembles around the great mahogany table, all expensive suits, anxious smiles, and greed hidden beneath professional language. And then the Televisor flickers on.

The Old Man appears on the screen.

His face is ghastly — one side slack, eyes only half open, mouth frozen and useless. Electrodes and probes are punched into his skull somewhere below, and a machine is now reading his thoughts, translating them into a cold synthetic voice. It is grotesque, humiliating, and somehow perfectly suited to him. He cannot move. He cannot speak. And yet the instant he appears, he begins savaging everyone in the room exactly as before.

One by one, he unleashes his usual daily venom. He insults the board members, accuses the attorneys of treachery, threatens to destroy each executive in terms tailored specifically to them, and then lands on his favorites. He snarls at Nedry that he will rip his guts out. He promises Bella he will tear her head off. And when poor Nadine makes the mistake of standing too stiffly at her desk, he even finds time to sneer that if she does not shut her mouth and breathe through her nose for a change, he is going to paddle her behind.

Even reduced to a face on a screen, the Old Man still dominates the room.

When the board meeting finally ends and the executives file out, only Bella, Nedry, Red, Nadine, and Junior remain. Nedry and Bella take their stand. They tell the Old Man that this farce cannot continue. He cannot run Gamora from a television screen. By law, by optics, by common sense, he must resign.

The Old Man responds with even greater fury now that outsiders are gone. He calls Bella cunning but stupid. He calls Nedry too dim-witted to inherit a bicycle, much less a pharmaceutical empire. He mocks their dead parents as addled hippies and drug-burned fools who somehow managed to breed defectives. And again come the threat phrases, hotter and crueler than ever: “I’ll tear your head off!” “I’ll rip your guts out!”

Then suddenly, mechanically, he laughs.

And he reveals what he has prepared.

The Televisor feed cuts away from his ruined face and shifts to the laboratory below, where Stebner appears. Stebner is a brilliant, bought scientist who answers to no one but the Old Man, and he despises Nedry and Bella with the oily contempt of a servant who knows he serves the true master. Beside him stands something enormous: The Proxy.

It is a towering mechanical horror, part riot cop, part industrial war machine, like RoboCop fused with a nightmare. Steam leaks from its joints. Sparks spit from exposed seams. Its clawed hands are too big and too strong for precision. In place of a head is another Televisor screen. As Stebner demonstrates its controls, the machine tries to lift a simple box and crushes it to splinters.

Nedry is stunned and demands to know where the company found the money for such a monstrosity. The Old Man’s machine-voice answers with thunderous pride, boasting of corruption as if it were genius: the bribes, the bought senators, the government contracts, the mandated vaccines, the whole diseased machinery of Gamora’s empire. He declares that he will operate The Proxy from below and attend every board meeting through it until Nedry and Bella are dust in the family tomb.

Nedry screams at Stebner to shut it down. But Stebner calmly reminds them that they have no authority over him. He answers only to the Old Man. To prove it, the Old Man turns even on Stebner, warning that if he ever disobeys, he will stomp him into the pavement.

The screen goes dark. Nedry explodes in rage, seizing a heavy letter opener like a dagger. But Red places a gloved hand on his arm and, with quiet menace, tells him to get himself a drink and let him handle it.

Some time later, Bella and Nedry drift back into the office from opposite sides — one returning by the staircase, the other from the elevator — carrying themselves now with a strange new confidence. They switch the Televisor back on and confront the Old Man again. This time they do not merely argue. They confess. Smoothly, smugly, almost triumphantly, they admit that yes, they poisoned him. Yes, they meant for him to die. Yes, they were tired of waiting.

The Old Man roars with machine-translated fury, declaring that he suspected as much, that they will pay, that he will tear Bella’s head off and rip Nedry’s guts out if it takes him from beyond the grave—

And then he stops.

Because two gloved hands suddenly rise into frame on the Televisor and grip the Old Man’s face from behind.

The murder happens right there on the screen.

There is no cutaway, no mercy. His helpless face twists in agony, blood leaking from his eyes, nose, and mouth as the unseen killer crushes the life out of him. And by the angle, the gloves, the shape moving just beyond the edge of the frame, we understand exactly what Nedry and Bella understand:

It is Red.

Just as planned.

The Televisor is switched off. Silence hangs in the office for one awful beat, and then Bella delivers the first line, cool as ice: “I guess he died in his sleep, poor bastard.”

Nedry, clutching his drink, says that he got what he deserved.

Then Bella lifts her glass and says, with wicked irony she does not understand, “And now we’re going to get what we deserve.”

They toast.

And then the building shudders.

A deep booming impact rolls up from below. Nadine looks up and wonders aloud if it is thunder. Junior, frightened by loud noises, edges closer to her desk. Then comes another boom, stronger this time, from somewhere in the belly of the building. Nedry mutters, “What are they up to now?” and flips the Televisor through the internal security channels: an empty office, another empty office, the lobby, Nadine’s desk, the medical room where the Old Man’s corpse still lies. Then the lab.

“The Proxy… it’s gone,” Bella whispers.

At first the camera cannot focus. Then, horribly, the foreground resolves, and Stebner’s body comes into view — flattened into the laboratory floor exactly as the Old Man had promised, stomped into the pavement.

Bella drops her drink and bolts for the staircase, shoving Junior to the carpet in her panic.

The booming grows louder. The room trembles. Ceiling dust sifts down. Glass rattles. Sparks dance in the air.

Then The Proxy appears at the top of the stairs.

It is worse now than before. Steam bursts from its joints. Its giant feet crack the marble. Static electricity crawls over its metal body like an aurora. On its Televisor head is not Stebner, but the dead face of the Old Man himself, electrodes and probes still hanging from his ruined skull, his empty eyes staring from the screen like a mechanized ghost. And in one iron hand, tangled by her thick shrubbery of 80s hair, dangles Bella’s severed head.

Nadine’s glasses fall from her face as her eyes, nostrils, and mouth open in pure horror. She cannot even scream. Yet in the same instant, her first instinct is not to save herself, but to rush to Junior, helping him up and dragging him toward the elevator.

But Junior is slow. And The Proxy is fast.

With just three thunderous strides, it crosses the office. It raises the hand that is not carrying Bella’s head and, in a grotesque parody of the Old Man’s usual threat, swats Nadine hard across the buttocks — not to kill her, not even really to hurt her, but as if faithfully carrying out the petty cruelty once promised to her. The blow knocks her down, but it is not meant as murder.

Frantic, Nadine and Junior scramble into the elevator, mashing the button, turning in terror to face whatever comes next.

But the machine stops.

It pivots away from them.

Toward Nedry.

As the elevator doors begin to close, The Proxy lurches forward, steam roaring, and in the Old Man’s terrible voice it cries out again and again:

“I’ll rip out your guts! I’ll rip out your guts! I’ll rip—”

And then the doors seal shut.

The elevator descends.

And the closing narration tells us that in every age, power dreams of surviving the body that wields it. Through heirs, through machines, through corporations, through systems built to outlive conscience. But in *The Atomic Zone*, one old tyrant found the perfect vessel for his cruelty — a proxy made of steel, static, and unfinished business. And for those who thought murder was a shortcut to inheritance, the final lesson was simple: some men are too evil to die quietly, and some offices are too corrupt to be haunted by anything less than exactly what they deserve.
