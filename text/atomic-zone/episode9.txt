Not very long from now, when mankind still believed that every problem in the universe was just one more engineering challenge away from being solved, a ship left Earth for Alpha Centauri.

Not a warship. Not an ark. Not a desperate escape from some ruined planet.

An exploration vessel.

A sleek contemporary SpaceX Starship, stretched and modified into humanity’s first interstellar craft, built around an impossible engine that no one fully understood, but everyone was happy to believe in. A zero-point vacuum drive, or something equally optimistic and irresponsible. A machine that could push with a steady one G for years at a time, making gravity inside the ship feel as normal as home, while turning the black ocean between the stars into something a human lifetime could finally cross.

Three astronauts were placed in cryogenic sleep. Their mission was simple. Accelerate halfway to the nearest star. Turn the ship around. Decelerate for the rest of the trip. Reach another sun in about six years of Earth time, while the crew themselves would experience only about three and a half.

That was the plan.

And plans, in this region of existence, have a way of becoming archaeology.

The first thing they feel is cold.

The second thing they feel is weight.

And the third thing they feel is wrong.

Three cryogenic chambers unlatch at the same time. Their frost melts in the weak red emergency lighting. The air tastes metallic. The deck hums faintly under their feet. Somewhere in the walls, battery systems are carrying a load they were never meant to carry for long.

Their ship has lost main power.

Their sleep chambers failed together because the reactor is gone. The impossible engine that was supposed to burn for a century has gone silent. Emergency batteries are now running life support, lights, basic systems, and a low-thrust ion system to keep gravity from vanishing completely. Not enough to fly. Not enough to steer. Just enough to stop the human body from becoming a loose collection of fluids drifting through a dark tube.

They wake coughing, shivering, confused.

They should be nearing Alpha Centauri.

They should be preparing for insertion into orbit around another star.

Instead, when they look through the forward port, they see nothing.

Not darkness with stars in it.

Not distance.

Nothing.

No constellations. No sun. No glow of the Milky Way. No distant scatter of galaxies. No Earth beacon. No cosmic microwave background haze. No radiation alarms screaming about the universe outside.

Just black.

A black so perfect it doesn’t even feel like space.

At first they think the windows have polarized. Then they think the sensors are broken. Then they check the backups, and the backups tell them the same thing the windows do.

Outside the ship, there is no detectable universe.

So they do what modern astronauts always do when reality fails quality control.

They ask the AI.

The ship’s intelligence comes online in a calm, pleasant voice, as if the crew has just overslept breakfast and missed a routine systems briefing.

Good morning, crew.

Emergency power mode is active.

Breakfast has been prepared in the galley.

Steak, eggs, and hash browns.

There is a silence after that, the kind only a machine can misjudge.

Then the captain asks the question.

What happened?

And the AI, with the same tone one might use to report a stuck air filter, explains that the ship’s engine control assembly suffered a mechanical jam at the midpoint of the mission. The turnover burn was never initiated. The vessel did not rotate to begin deceleration. It continued under uninterrupted acceleration.

For one hundred shipboard years.

The crew stares at the log.

One hundred years.

Not the three and a half they were meant to sleep through. Not six years as measured on Earth. One hundred years inside the ship, with the engine maintaining constant proper acceleration, gravity always normal, clocks always ordinary, while the outside universe slipped into relativistic insanity.

The AI continues.

At one year of ship time, velocity had reached roughly seventy-seven percent of light speed. Earth had aged about one point two years. The ship had already crossed more than half a light-year.

At two years, it was moving at ninety-six point eight percent of light speed. The nearest stars were behind them. Earth had aged nearly four years.

At five years, the ship had crossed more than eighty light-years, while Earth had aged more than eighty years. The stars ahead were compressing into a bright circular patch. The stars behind were fading red and dying into radio silence.

At ten years, over fourteen thousand years had passed outside. The ship had crossed a distance measured in fourteen thousand light-years. Humanity’s oldest civilizations had risen and vanished in the time it took the hull to warm and cool across a few maintenance cycles.

At twelve years, the vessel had crossed the width of the Milky Way.

At twenty years, the ship was hundreds of millions of light-years from Earth’s long-dead coordinates, while nearly half a billion years had passed in the outside frame.

At fifty years, the number was no longer a history.

It was geology for galaxies.

At one hundred years of elapsed time aboard ship, the universe outside had aged approximately three point two nine times ten to the forty-fourth years.

The AI says this the way a flight attendant might mention mild turbulence.

The crew asks what it saw.

And the AI tells them.

Early in the voyage, the stars had seemed to slide forward, as if the whole sky were being gathered into the direction of travel. Constellations warped. Light from ahead shifted blue. Light from behind shifted red.

Then the effect became extreme. Nearly all visible structure crowded into a shrinking disk dead ahead, brilliant and violent. Ordinary starlight blueshifted beyond visibility, then beyond safety. The cosmic background itself became a forward blaze. Behind the ship, the universe dimmed, reddened, and fell away. A dark horizon formed aft, swallowing everything that could no longer reach them.

The ship ran on.

The forward disk grew smaller and more lethal. The aft darkness grew larger and more absolute. Over ages impossible to summarize meaningfully for biological minds, stars burned out, galaxies dispersed, black holes decayed, and every recognizable source of structure thinned toward silence.

The AI pauses, then says it can no longer correlate external readings with any known physical model.

There are no stars because there are no stars left.

There is no Earth beacon because there is no Earth.

There is no cosmic radiation because there is no cosmos in any measurable thermodynamic sense consistent with the vessel’s surviving reference library.

There is no direction because direction requires relation.

There is no motion because motion requires elsewhere.

There is no outside.

One of them laughs.

It is not a healthy laugh.

Another asks the AI if the windows are still transparent.

Yes, the AI says. The windows are functioning within nominal parameters.

But now they appear as mirrors.

Not because of glare. Not because of tint. Not even because of the ordinary partial reflection at a glass surface, that little Fresnel trick by which light decides some of it will continue on and some of it will turn back.

No.

Now they are mirrors because there is nowhere for light to go.

No stars to scatter against. No space to carry contrast. No distant black to absorb a gaze. The universe beyond the glass has become so empty, so final, that the only image left for the ship to see is itself.

Three old astronauts in a dead vessel.

An AI with excellent manners.

And a galley serving breakfast at the end of time.

The AI asks whether it should reduce power to the ion thrusters. At the current output, battery reserves will provide approximately seven more hours of Earth-normal gravity. Reducing simulated gravity to fifty percent would extend operating life by an additional three hours.

Would the crew like to do that?

Would the crew like music?

Would the crew like coffee?

And that is the cruel little masterpiece of it all.

Not the death of the stars.

Not the evaporation of history.

Not even the possibility that these three accidental voyagers have outrun causality itself and arrived in a place where the words place, when, and after have all become fossils.

No.

The cruelest thing is breakfast.

Steak.

Eggs.

Hash browns.

A hot meal plated by machine hands while every cathedral, every battlefield, every love affair, every child’s drawing on every refrigerator door in every civilization that ever learned to say the word tomorrow has already been erased by time so thoroughly that even the ashes have expired.

And here they are.

At the last table.

At the last morning.

Held to the floor by a failing whisper of engineered gravity, in a ship that crossed farther than meaning itself could follow, staring into windows that have become perfect black mirrors, waiting for the batteries to surrender and for up and down to become old superstitions.

Maybe they talk.

Maybe they eat.

Maybe one of them asks for music.

Maybe the AI, still faithful to its final duty, finds one last song to play for the only audience left in existence.

But in The Atomic Zone, there is only one ending that really matters right now.

The end of this video.

Goodbye.
