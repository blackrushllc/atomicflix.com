Episode 5 — The Man Who Came Back

By the start of this final episode, the storm and the iceberg are behind John Rowland, but the hardest part of his story is not yet over. He has survived the Titan, protected little Myra on the ice, refused to profit from legal revenge in London, and sworn off drink forever. Now he is sailing back west with the child in his care, carrying the one living proof that something good came out of the wreck. He thinks only of returning her to her mother. He imagines gratitude, surprise, perhaps even a kind of peace. But Robertson is too bitter, and too observant, to let the story end so easily.

On the crossing home, Rowland realizes he never sent word ahead that the child is safe. Meanwhile, the insurance men in London decide at last to release his story to the press, hoping publicity will stir up corroborating testimony from other survivors. The account is embellished as it spreads, especially the part about the polar bear, and it is cabled across the Atlantic with the name of the ship carrying Rowland home. But the news arrives in New York too late to help him before he walks down the gangplank with little Myra on his shoulder.

That delay matters, because what follows is one of the cruelest ironies in the whole novella.

Rowland lands not as a celebrated rescuer but as a ragged, one-armed seaman carrying a fashionable child through the streets of Manhattan. Before he goes to Mrs. Selfridge, he does one tender, practical thing first: he spends the last of his money at a department store to have the little girl bathed, dressed, and made presentable, because, as he sees it, she is a Fifth Avenue child and ought to be restored in dignity. It is a beautiful moment because it shows how complete his transformation has become. He does not think of himself at all. He is still in rags; he is still penniless; but the child must look right when she goes home.

And then society does what society so often does: it sees appearances and leaps instantly to accusation.

A policeman notices the contrast between the ragged man and the finely dressed child and stops him. Rowland answers with too much pride, too little tact, and almost at once suspicion hardens into force. At that exact moment Myra’s mother appears, sees Rowland with the child, screams, snatches the girl away, and collapses in hysterics while her father denounces Rowland as a scoundrel and kidnapper. The child herself is crying for Rowland, but her voice does not matter. The crowd sides with wealth, respectability, and panic. The policeman clubs Rowland and drags him off through the street like a criminal. In one savage stroke, the man who saved the child from shipwreck, cold, hunger, and a polar bear is reduced, in civilized New York, to a public monster.

That scene is the key to the ending because it strips away the romance of reunion and replaces it with something harsher and more modern: reputation is social, not moral. The truth is real, but until institutions recognize it, the truth is helpless. Rowland has become a better man than almost anyone in the story, yet he is treated according to his clothing, his poverty, and old fears attached to his name. Mrs. Selfridge still sees the same dangerous unbeliever and rejected suitor she feared years before. Her father still sees insult to family honor. The police see only a likely culprit. The child alone responds honestly.

Chapter XV turns this humiliation into something almost satirical. Rowland is brought into court bruised, disheveled, and treated as just another miserable offender. The prosecution repeats all the old poison: that he once courted Mrs. Selfridge, that he threatened revenge, that he followed her aboard ship “in the guise of a sailor,” and that he even attempted harm to the child. It is a grotesque rewriting of everything we have seen. But Robertson very cleverly stages the reversal not through eloquence from Rowland, who barely defends himself, but through journalism. Reporters in the courtroom, recognizing the name from the morning papers, get the article in front of the magistrate. The court reads, notices the empty sleeve, connects the prisoner to the iceberg rescue, and in an instant the case collapses. Rowland is discharged on the spot.

It is a deeply ironic acquittal. He is not saved by the people who know him, and not even by the mother whose child he preserved. He is saved by print culture, by chance timing, by a newspaper story dramatic enough to break through the laziness and prejudice of the courtroom. Even then, Mrs. Selfridge leaves more mystified than repentant, while little Myra cries out for Rowland as he slips away from the reporters and disappears back into the anonymous city. There is no immediate embrace, no public crowning of the hero, no restoration scene fit for melodrama. Robertson refuses all of that.

Then comes Chapter XVI, and with it one of the most quietly satisfying codas in the book.

Rowland starts over from nothing. He fishes under the docks. He trades fish for meals. He buys secondhand clothes. He gets work tallying cargo, then addressing envelopes, then sits for a Civil Service examination. Slowly, stubbornly, and without spectacle, he rebuilds himself. This is one of the strongest parts of the ending because it makes redemption practical. He is not magically restored to naval rank or suddenly welcomed back into elite society. He simply works. He becomes sober, respectable, employable, and once again visibly a gentleman. Two years later he is appointed to a good government position and tells himself that his future is finally his own, and that his old suffering came from overvaluing women and whisky.

But even that is not the end, because the book’s final note is smaller and softer than all the calamity that came before. After six more months, Rowland receives a letter from Mrs. Selfridge. She says she has watched from a distance while he fought his way back to his old standards. She congratulates him. And then she admits the true reason for writing: little Myra will not forget him. The child keeps asking for him and crying. Mrs. Selfridge can bear it no longer and asks whether he will come and see Myra. Robertson closes with a perfect last stroke: “And the man went to see—Myra.” The ambiguity is deliberate, of course. He goes to see the child, but the line also lets the older Myra echo behind the younger one, so that love, gratitude, memory, and second chances all hang in the air without being explained away.

And that makes the ending much better than a simple happy ending.

The Titan is gone. Hundreds are dead. Institutions proved corrupt, callous, or absurd. The mother never fully understands what Rowland truly was. The world does not neatly reward virtue. And yet something real has still happened: a broken man has chosen truth, sacrifice, sobriety, courage, and responsibility, again and again, until he has become worthy of the life he nearly lost. In a story obsessed with steel, speed, wealth, and prestige, Robertson finally locates greatness somewhere else entirely — in character under pressure. The Titan was called unsinkable and went down. Rowland was called ruined and rose.
