Episode 4 — Lloyd’s, Law, and the Price of Truth

The story now does something wonderfully unexpected: after the ice, the screams, the overturned decks, and the lonely struggle for life on the berg, it turns away from the sea and goes straight into the machinery of civilization. We are brought into London, into the Royal Exchange, into Lloyd’s, into that hard bright world where shipwreck becomes chalked bulletins, legal clauses, ruined investors, and men trying to calculate exactly what a human catastrophe will cost. In Robertson’s hands, this is not a cooling-off period after the wreck. It is another form of drama altogether — colder, more civilized, and in some ways even more ruthless.

Lloyd’s is presented almost like a second ocean: crowded, noisy, technical, relentless, and global. Every captain’s record is known there, every casualty is tracked, every rumor becomes actionable intelligence. Families come there in grief for news, while underwriters and brokers come there in dread of what the news will do to their fortunes. The sinking of the Titan does not merely break hearts; it sends financial shock through an entire system of men whose business is to turn danger into numbers. And among them, one of the loudest and most desperate is Mr. Meyer, the underwriter whose personal exposure to the Titan is so heavy that the loss threatens to ruin him.

Robertson is very sharp here about how tragedy stratifies itself. While one old man returns again and again asking after his missing son and grandchild, Meyer drinks, rages, and calculates. Neither man is untouched by the wreck, but each suffers in a different currency. The old gentleman, Mr. Selfridge, is losing blood family, legacy, and the last emotional stake of his life. Meyer is losing money, status, solvency. The novel does not deny the reality of financial ruin, but it makes us feel the moral awkwardness of a world in which an underwriter may cry out “this will ruin me” at the same disaster that has drowned hundreds.

Then comes the hinge of the whole episode: John Rowland is on his way to London, alive, carrying the child. That fact electrifies the insurance struggle because Rowland is no longer merely a survivor. He is evidence. He is the one man who knows what happened on the Titan before the wreck — the collision with the Royal Age, the attempted bribery, the drugging, the use of a compromised lookout, and the full speed recklessness that had always been disguised as confidence. Meyer knows that if Rowland’s testimony can be shaped the right way, the insurance may be voided. Captain Bryce and the company attorney know that if Rowland speaks freely, the company may be morally and legally damned. So when they all gather in Meyer’s office, the room becomes a kind of courtroom before any court has convened.

The confrontation is one of the best scenes in the novella. Bryce and Austen arrive sleek, polished, and outwardly respectable, trying to preserve the fiction that the voyage had been uneventful until the iceberg. Meyer, oily but shrewd, begins pressing them with just enough legal knowledge to make them sweat, then springs the accusation: they concealed the collision with the Royal Age. Their answer is exactly what men in power so often reach for when cornered — attack the witness. Rowland is called an irresponsible drunkard; his mind is questioned; his condition is exaggerated. Yet this time he does not stand alone. Captain Barry, the man who rescued him from the berg and knows what sort of courage he possesses, is there beside him, and his blunt loyalty gives the whole scene a satisfying moral force.

What makes these chapters so good is that the moral argument turns into a legal argument without losing any dramatic power. At first the attorney coolly says that even if the Royal Age story is true, it does not by itself void the policy. But then Meyer tries another angle: if Rowland was drunk on lookout when the Titan struck the iceberg, perhaps the insurers can escape payment on grounds of bad faith or unlawful conduct. That is when the trap reverses. Rowland, thinking more clearly than any of them, asks whether Bryce and Austen were part owners. When it is admitted that they were, he lays out the real issue: if these part owners drugged a sailor, placed him on lookout, and continued under such reckless conditions, then their own unlawful acts as owner-officers may have nullified the insurance. In one move, the broken sailor becomes the sharpest mind in the room.

There is also a grim pleasure in watching the hierarchy collapse. Captain Bryce, who had once towered over Rowland with authority, can answer only with rage. Captain Barry floors him with a punch when he advances threateningly, Austen begins trimming his story to save himself, and Meyer realizes that his salvation may be near. By the end of Chapter XII, the officers who had tried to destroy Rowland are no longer in command of a liner; they are frightened men in an office, staring at exposure, disgrace, and possible criminal pursuit. Meyer all but tells them to flee England under assumed names before Lloyd’s bulletins and Scotland Yard close in. The same civilized world that once gave them rank and prestige is now ready to spit them out.

Then Robertson adds one more cruel turn: Mr. Selfridge, who has only half understood the technical struggle around him, asks the simple question that matters most to him — will the insurance be paid? Meyer, in triumph, tells him no, and explains that the loss will fall on him. The shock is fatal. Before a doctor can arrive, Selfridge dies there in the office. It is a hard scene because there is no villainous flourish to it; there is only a frail old man struck down by the sudden union of grief and ruin. And standing near him is Rowland, who has by now become protector not just of the child’s body but of her future.

This is where Episode 4 reaches its real emotional summit. Rowland learns that if he gives the testimony Meyer wants, the insurance will be defeated, the Titan investors will be ruined, and little Myra herself will inherit catastrophe instead of security. So he does the one thing nobody in that office expects: he refuses to testify. He lets the insurance stand. It is an extraordinary act, because it does not erase Bryce’s guilt or Meyer’s opportunism, and it does not pretend the system is just. Rowland simply chooses the child over vengeance. He decides that if the underwriters knowingly accepted the risk of a full-speed Atlantic racer in fog and ice, then they must bear the loss they priced into their business. In that sense, he is still serving justice — but now a broader and more merciful justice than the room around him can understand.

Meyer’s reaction is almost comic in its desperation: pleading, wailing, locking the door, trying to bully and coax Rowland into saving him. But the scene ends not with legal triumph, but with human loyalty. Barry stands by Rowland completely. He helps him leave, helps secure his wages and passage, and understands at once why Rowland made the choice: he would not break the child in order to punish the guilty. That may be the deepest thing about Rowland in this whole middle stretch of the novel. He does not become saintly. He becomes responsible. He is no longer acting from wounded pride, or even chiefly from outrage, but from a future-oriented compassion that the polished men of law and insurance seem incapable of imagining.

So Episode 4 ends in a very different register from Episode 3. There is no iceberg now, no polar bear, no frozen moonlight. Instead there is London, paper, liability, stock, testimony, and the revelation that the world after disaster may be every bit as pitiless as the disaster itself. Yet out of that world Rowland emerges morally stronger than ever: poorer, injured, still socially ruined perhaps, but now clearly the noblest figure in the story. And with passage secured and little Myra still in his care, the tale is ready to cross one final threshold — back to America, back to the mother, and into the last emotional reckoning of all.
