On a June morning in the Midwestern industrial town of Middletown, scientist John Kenniston is walking down Mill Street toward the Industrial Research Laboratories where he works. Without warning, the sky explodes in a blinding flash and the ground heaves violently beneath him. Kenniston immediately realizes that the long-feared atomic war has begun and that a super-atomic bomb has detonated overhead. Expecting devastation, he struggles back to his feet—only to discover that the town appears completely unchanged. Buildings still stand, people are still in the streets, and aside from the shock of the explosion, nothing seems damaged.

Kenniston and a stunned policeman try to make sense of what has happened. The officer insists the bomb must have been a harmless “fizzle,” but Kenniston begins to notice strange details: the sunlight is dim and reddish, the air has turned unnaturally cold, and faint stars are visible even in the daytime sky. When he looks up, Kenniston realizes with horror that the Sun itself looks wrong—no longer the bright golden star of Earth, but a dull red orb that can be stared at directly. Uneasy but determined to find answers, he continues on to the laboratory.

At the Industrial Research Laboratories, Kenniston meets the other scientists, including his superior Dr. Hubble. Communications with the outside world have mysteriously failed—telephone lines and radio transmissions bring no response from anywhere beyond Middletown. Hubble studies the strange sky and performs calculations based on the positions of the stars. Meanwhile, a frightened local electrician reports that part of his farmland outside town has simply vanished, replaced by something unrecognizable. These disturbing clues deepen the mystery and suggest that the world outside Middletown may have been radically altered.

Climbing the town’s water tower to look beyond the city limits, Kenniston and Hubble see the terrible truth. The familiar farmland surrounding Middletown has disappeared, replaced by a barren, alien landscape of yellow plains and strange hills beneath a darkened sky. From the altered positions of the stars and the aged appearance of the Sun, Hubble concludes that the super-atomic explosion did not merely destroy anything—it shattered space and time itself. Middletown, intact but isolated, has been hurled millions of years into Earth’s distant future, into a dying world where all the rest of humanity may already be gone.

City at World's End is a 1951 science fiction novel by Edmond Hamilton where a modern American city, Middletown, is mysteriously transported a million years into Earth's far future by a nuclear explosion, forcing its inhabitants to adapt to a desolate, alien world and confront their own descendants, who have become a galactic empire and view Earth as a museum planet. The story follows the citizens as they struggle to survive, explore advanced alien technology, and deal with the ethical dilemma of repopulating their ancestral home, exploring themes of survival, adaptation, and the consequences of nuclear war.
